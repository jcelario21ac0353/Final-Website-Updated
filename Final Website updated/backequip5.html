What is Seated Row Machine

The Seated Row Machine is a gym apparatus designed to target and strengthen your back muscles, particularly your latissimus dorsi (lats) and to a lesser extent your biceps. Unlike free weight rows with dumbbells or barbells, it offers a controlled movement path and a supported seat, making it ideal for beginners or those recovering from injuries. By adjusting the chest pad and handle positions, you can customize the exercise to target different areas of your back. With proper form, you can effectively pull the weight towards your chest, mimicking the rowing motion but with added stability and support.

How to Use

To master the Seated Row Machine, first adjust the chest pad for a comfortable distance from the handles. Sit with your back straight and core engaged, grasping the handles with a neutral or overhand grip. Initiate the movement by pulling the handles towards your chest, squeezing your shoulder blades together at the peak. Control the descent as you return the handles to the starting position, maintaining a slight bend in your elbows throughout the entire rep. Choose a weight that allows you to perform 10-15 controlled repetitions with good form.

DO's

  • Set the chest pad for a comfortable distance that allows full range of motion without straining your shoulders. Choose a handle grip (neutral or overhand) that feels natural.
  • Keep your back straight with a natural arch in your lower back, core engaged, and shoulders down throughout the movement.
  • Pull the handles towards your chest using your back muscles, squeezing your shoulder blades together at the top. Control the descent as you return the handles to the starting position.
  • Pull the handles towards your chest until you feel a good contraction in your lats, but avoid hyperextending your back at the top. Allow your elbows to bend slightly throughout the movement.
  • Start with a lighter weight and gradually increase it as you get stronger, prioritizing good form over heavier weights..

DON'Ts

  • This can put strain on your lower back. Maintain a neutral spine.
  • Keep your shoulders relaxed and down, focusing on pulling with your back muscles rather than shrugging.
  • The movement should be powered by your back muscles, not by rocking your body back and forth.
  • Maintain a slight bend in your knees for stability throughout the exercise.
  • Breathe steadily throughout the exercise, exhaling as you pull the weight and inhaling as you lower it.
  • If you struggle to maintain proper form, reduce the weight.
  • Focus on using your back muscles to move the weight, not momentum.
